Case studies are not just proof assets. They are decision aids. Buyers use them to understand whether you have solved a similar problem before and whether your process feels transferable to their situation.

## Put them where doubt appears

If a service page makes a strong claim, the reader will look for evidence close by. Linking directly to a relevant case study is usually more persuasive than sending every reader to a generic testimonials page.

## Write for the question behind the search

Many people search case studies because they want to know what happened, not just what was delivered. The useful structure is usually challenge, approach, outcome, and what changed after the work was complete.

## Keep the outcome language specific

Specificity helps both trust and retrieval. "Created alignment across teams" is weaker than "reduced review loops from five stakeholders to two decision-makers."
